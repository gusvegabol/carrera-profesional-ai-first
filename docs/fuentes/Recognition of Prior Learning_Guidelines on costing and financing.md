� Foreword
Technological advances, climate changes, and demographic shifts are fundamentally reshaping the
world of work. To navigate these changes and foster a just transition, investing in skills and lifelong
learning is essential. While people gain valuable competencies throughout their lives, this learning often
occurs outside formal systems. Without formal recognition, these skills remain invisible, hindering access
to decent work, lifelong learning opportunities, and social inclusion. Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL)
is the strategic tool that makes this vital learning visible.
Across the world, countries see RPL as essential for building resilient and inclusive workforces. It is a key
enabler for certifying the skills of workers in the informal economy, migrant workers, forcibly displaced
persons, and other vulnerable groups, thereby improving employability and promoting full, productive,
and freely chosen employment. For enterprises, RPL offers a pathway to validate and build upon the skills
of their employees, enhancing productivity and fostering economic development. However, while the
value of RPL is clear, the challenge of how to design innovative, transparent, equitable, and sustainably
finance it has remained a persistent obstacle, especially for low- and middle-income countries.
These guidelines are the ILO’s direct response to that challenge. They provide a comprehensive
framework for understanding and mastering the financial architecture of RPL, detailing the direct,
support, and opportunity costs involved. Moving beyond theory, this document explores diverse
financing models and provides practical guidance on building the sustainable, inclusive, and equitable
financial arrangements required for robust national systems. They are designed to support policymakers,
social partners, practitioners, and international organizations in making the informed financial decisions
necessary to build RPL systems that last.

Executive summary
These guidelines on costing and financing Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL) aim to fill the knowledge
gap in the financial aspects of establishing and maintaining RPL systems. They support countries,
especially low- and middle-income countries and those hosting migrant workers and displaced persons,
in developing, expanding and sustaining their RPL systems.
RPL systems are essential for certifying skills acquired through non-formal and informal learning.
These systems benefit individuals, especially those in the informal economy or disadvantaged groups,
by improving employability and career prospects. Enterprises also benefit from RPL as it allows their
employees to validate their skills.1
Key stakeholders in RPL systems include governments, adult learning centres, non-governmental
organizations, enterprises, certification bodies, social partners and individuals. Effective governance and
collaboration among these stakeholders are crucial for the success of RPL systems.
These guidelines detail the various costs associated with setting up and maintaining RPL systems,
categorized into direct costs, support costs and opportunity costs. They encompass awareness and
information dissemination, guidance and facilitation, assessment and certification, upskilling, as well as
the regulatory and institutional framework, stakeholder engagement and quality assurance.
Financing RPL involves multiple models and funding sources, including government funding, employer
contributions, individual payments and donor financing. These guidelines discuss the balance between
demand-side and supply-side funding, emphasizing the importance of sustainable, inclusive and equitable
financial arrangements.
Effective governance structures are necessary to ensure transparency, accountability and efficient use
of resources in financing RPL. This includes establishing clear regulatory frameworks, quality assurance
mechanisms and engaging stakeholders through promotion and communication strategies.
Common challenges in implementing RPL systems include securing long-term financing ensuring
government and enterprise involvement, reaching remote and disadvantaged groups, ensuring quality
assurance and avoiding reliance on a single funding source. These guidelines recommend adopting
measures to save costs such as leveraging existing infrastructure, promoting flexible delivery schedules
and establishing partnerships with enterprises and existing education and training centres with technical
platforms.
The document provides various country examples, including Australia, Bangladesh, Colombia, Kenya,
South Africa and Tunisia, illustrating different approaches to costing and financing RPL. These examples
highlight the importance of tailored solutions to address specific national contexts and needs.
Finally, these guidelines emphasize the need for comprehensive planning, stakeholder engagement and
sustainable financial arrangements to establish and maintain effective RPL systems. These guidelines
focus on the financial aspects of RPL to help countries enhance workforce skills, improve employability
and promote lifelong learning.

Introduction
While assessing and recognizing skills that people already possess is expected to be less costly than
developing these skills, Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL) systems do have a cost. Many countries have
described the financing mechanisms they have opted for, as well as the different services included in
an RPL system that need to be developed and maintained. Yet there is often little knowledge about the
costs for setting up and sustaining such a system. A deeper understanding of financing models and
their effectiveness in achieving the different purposes of RPL required to inform policymakers, guide the
decision-making processes and facilitate the development of tailored solutions that support systems’
longevity and equitable participation.
These new ILO guidelines aim to fill this gap by providing evidence-based advice on how to assess costs
and design financing arrangements to set up, expand and/or sustain a country’s RPL system.2 While
experiences of countries in multiple income groups will be taken into consideration, the guide aims to
primarily support low- and middle-income countries and those that host migrant workers and forcibly
displaced persons. For practical purposes, only six countries were studied in detail during the fact-finding
process. Australia, Bangladesh, Colombia, Kenya, South Africa and Tunisia chosen as a representative
sample, from different languages, income levels, regions of the world and at different stages of
advancement regarding RPL. The methodology involved a mix of desk-based research and interviews
with key respondents whenever necessary for clarification in relation to the existing literature.
In addition to this introduction, an executive summary, a list of references and some annexes, this
document contains five sections. Section 1 provides some guidance on how to use these guidelines, for
readers who want to go straight to their topic(s) of interest and/or read the sections independently of
one another. Section 2 provides a brief reminder of what RPL is about. Section 3 describes all the possible
costs of RPL. Section 4 analyses the RPL financial arrangements. Section 5 proposes a conclusion with a
costing checklist.
� How to use these guidelines
This guide has been designed to support ILO constituents wishing to develop RPL in their own country and
to help sustain the RPL system in countries that have already piloted and/or implemented it.
This tool shows how to assess costs and design financing arrangements to set up and sustain a country’s
RPL system and to determine how costs should be shared among the key stakeholders in the system
(governments, employers, individuals and international organizations). This guide will help to consider the
different contextual factors in setting up the system, the costs of implementation and the sustainability

� 1.1. Defining RPL and its target groups
The ILO defines the term “recognition of prior learning” as a process, undertaken by qualified
personnel, of identifying, documenting, assessing and certifying a person’s competences,
acquired through formal, non-formal or informal learning, based on established qualification
standards.3
RPL helps compensate for the lack of formal qualifications at the end of initial education
and/or work-based learning, often in the informal economy and supports access to further
occupational and educational opportunities. It also enables people to upgrade their skills and
qualifications or requalify, through appropriate assessments and/or additional training, often
in a shorter time frame than formal learning systems.
RPL also widens access to lifelong learning, particularly for disadvantaged groups such as
migrant workers and people with low levels of qualification.
� 1.2. Benefits of RPL for individuals and socie-
ties
The RPL system offers a wide range of benefits for all stakeholders in a lifelong learning system,
with these benefits varying depending on the country’s specific context. These benefits are
typically linked to educational, socio-political and economic advantages for societies.4
RPL contributes to the development of more flexible and adaptable education and training
systems by validating learning outcomes acquired in formal, non-formal and informal contexts.
It can lead to full or part qualifications, credits toward a qualification or exemption for access
to formal education, thereby supporting upskilling and occupational mobility.5 This flexibility
allows less traditional learners, such as those from low socio-economic backgrounds or
older individuals, to access higher levels of education more efficiently, reducing the time and
resources needed to obtain qualifications and enabling smoother career transitions.
RPL’s ability to grant access and new opportunities for disadvantaged groups positions it as
a vital tool for promoting social inclusion and equity. By officially validating an individual’s
knowledge and skills, RPL enhances employability and provides formal recognition of a certain
level of competence. In turn, this recognition empowers individuals to leverage their certified
skills in the labour market in various ways. For instance, RPL can help meet qualification
requirements in regulated occupations, support transitions between sectors and facilitate the
transfer of knowledge, skills and competences across national borders, thereby enhancing skills
portability and mobility for migrant workers. RPL also enables transitions from the informal to

� 1.3. Key stakeholders of RPL systems
RPL services are inherently shared, involving collaboration across multiple sectors and stakeholders to
ensure effective implementation and broader societal impact (see also section 3 on the shared nature of
RPL services).
The following list outlines the primary stakeholders who contribute to and are impacted by the RPL
process:
� governments may provide the framework for RPL, drive the political agenda and determine the
relevance of RPL and regulate the roles of other stakeholders involved in RPL;
� ministries responsible for certification and curriculum development. They will create or adap t
standards and curricula, enabling assessment and selection of the right qualification for the
candidate;
� adult learning centres and their respective staff, such as trainers and assessors. They can be private
(for example, training centres), public (for example, the public employment service) or both. They
are in charge of the information on RPL, offer guidance to applicants and carry out the RPL process,
usually identifying, documenting and assessing skills;
� non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and donors. They will play a role in financing and assisting
with implementation;
� employers are also involved in financing and assessment;
� employers’ and workers’ organizations, play an important role in system design, implementation, as
well as monitoring and evaluation;
� individuals who are seeking recognition of prior learning are at the heart of the system.
� 1.4. Common challenges in RPL implementation
Most RPL implementation challenges are identical in the different regions. They are particularly present
in the countries studied and are detailed below. There are other challenges that are more specific to each
country, but they are not detailed here for the sake of brevity.
� Long-term financing: In many countries, such as Morocco and Tunisia, the cost of RPL has not been
taken into account over the long-term. It seems necessary to anticipate the costs of generalizing the
process following the pilots.
� Government involvement: Government support is very important to sustain the widespread
introduction of RPL. It seems essential for the State to play an active role in implementing RPL. Some
countries have begun to implement RPL thanks to the support (for example, financial, expertise )of
NGOs. or international development partners. In Morocco and Tunisia, despite the deployment of
initial pilot programmes, full-scale implementation has stalled due to a lack of sustained financial and
political commitment from the governments.
� Reaching remote workers. In all countries, Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET)
policies come up against the difficulty of reaching the most disadvantaged groups, including with RP L
can serve as an effective tool to support these individuals (see section 2.2) but to successfully reach
these audiences, it is important to invest in communication and deploy strategies tailored to their
specific needs.

� Mono-financing: Some countries, such as Bangladesh, finance the RPL system with money from
international organizations and NGOs. In the event of a subsidy cut-off or reduction, countries need
to be able to anticipate and envisage alternative funding.
� Quality assurance: The quality of the system is essential if qualifications are to be recognized by the
general public and employers.
� Developing the reference framework for qualifications: The RPL system relies on assessing
the competences required for a given qualification, which means that a reference framework fo r
each qualification is essential. This framework serves as the foundation for RPL implementation .
Each country can then decide which qualifications are eligible for RPL. For example, in France ,
qualifications eligible for RPL must be registered with theR épertoire national des certifications
professionnelles (RNCP). Certain qualifications, such as nursing, cannot be validated through RPL.
� Ensuring the value and recognition of qualifications: Importantly, when a qualification can
be awarded through RPL, its value and recognition in the labour market must be equivalent to
those obtained through formal education and training systems. This requires RPL providers to be
recognized by the formal sector to ensure the qualification’s value is upheld. In some cases, specifi c
economic sectors may have their own qualification systems that allow RPL, independent of the forma l
education system. The key factors here are: 1) the acceptance of the RPL qualification by the relevant
industry, ensuring it has value for both the individual and the labour market and 2) the alignment of
RPL qualifications with formal systems if the learning outcomes are equivalent.
� Working in parallel with a qualifications framework: The challenge with implementing
National Qualifications Frameworks (NQFs) is that they may result in RPL being predated by forma l
qualifications, thus potentially not meeting the actual needs of the labour market or reproducing
the same biases as the existing educational systems. While a qualifications framework is often
seen as the natural companion to the RPL system, it is not a sufficient or necessary condition. RP L
systems have long predated qualifications frameworks, sometimes by as much as 50 years. And while
qualification frameworks can help ensure equivalence of RPL qualifications and provide pathways
to further learning, they might also limit the value of RPL and its usefulness for labour markets,
perpetuating existing inequalities from the education system. In France, for example, the concept
of recognizing professional experience through official certification first emerged in 1934 with the
awarding of the graduate engineer title, long before the creation of the RNCP in 1969. In Canada, RPL
has been implemented across various sectors, institutions and government-sponsored programmes
at the federal, provincial and community levels since the 1970s, despite the absence of a national
qualifications framework.

The shared nature of RPL services across different institutions makes it challenging to fully
trace the associated costs and funding. Information on the costs is often scarce, and the lack of
comprehensive evidence hampers efforts to conduct cost–benefit analyses. While RPL funding
can be understood within specific, well-defined contexts, the overall picture at the national,
regional or local levels remains complex. Yet, a detailed understanding of costs is essential for
revealing potential efficiency gains from RPL, as well as for providing a basis for advocacy and
policy development.
This section seeks to fill these knowledge gaps by drawing on evidence from six country case
studies and examples across the world. It analyses the various costs associated with RPL,
categorizing them into direct costs, support costs and opportunity costs. The cost model
proposed here breaks down the RPL process into manageable components, allowing for
detailed measurement and analysis of costs at the level of individual activities. This framework
will assist stakeholders in understanding the financial implications of implementing RPL,
both in terms of the direct costs of facilitating the process and the support costs involved in
establishing and maintaining the overall system. It aims to equip policymakers and practitioners
with the tools to estimate and manage these costs, enabling more informed decisions about
cost-sharing and funding allocation. Ultimately, this model supports strategic planning for
scalability and promotes the long-term sustainability of RPL systems.
The analysis covers key stages of the RPL process (see figure 2), including awareness and
information dissemination, guidance and facilitation, assessment and certification, and
addressing skills gaps. Additionally, it addresses critical system building blocks identified by the
ILO (2015)8, such as regulatory and institutional frameworks, social dialogue and stakeholder
engagement, as well as quality assurance. For each component, the primary cost elements are
identified, analysed and cost-saving measures are suggested.

To facilitate understanding of the cost analysis, several key considerations are important to note. The costs
identified in this section do not always translate into direct expenditures. Many of these elements could
be covered by existing resources or through enhanced capacity within institutions, reducing the need for
new financial outlays. Understanding this distinction between theoretical costs and actual expenditure is
critical to fully assessing the financial impact of an RPL system.
Costs are categorized as either fixed – remaining constant regardless of the number of candidates – or
variable, changing with the scale of operations. However, as the RPL system scales up, this distinction can
become blurred. For instance, while a coordinator’s salary may remain a fixed cost, increasing the number
of assessments might necessitate hiring additional assessors and administrative staff, making staffing
a mixed cost that fluctuates based on capacity and demand. Therefore, in these guidelines, staff time is
treated as either fixed or mixed, depending on the situation.
Finally, while this cost model offers a high level of detail, it acknowledges that some assumptions must
be made and that contextual factors may affect cost estimates. Nevertheless, this framework serves as
a foundation for more accurate cost identification, helping stakeholders make informed decisions and
advocate for efficient and equitable RPL systems.
� 2.1. Direct costs: Implementing the RPL process
2.1.1. Awareness and information
RPL on a large scale is a relatively new and still evolving concept in most parts of the world. Many
stakeholders and potential beneficiaries are unaware of the opportunities that it offers in terms of
outcomes, process and benefits.
Without sound investments to raise awareness and disseminate information about RPL, potential
candidates might not know about opportunities to have their skills assessed and certified. Increased
costs may also arise down the line. For instance, an RPL pilot in Kenya revealed that the budget to
implement communication initiatives had been inadequate. Consequently, RPL personnel had to spend
time raising awareness during the screening phase instead of providing workers with detailed guidance
and counselling for subsequent stages, which should have been their principal role.10
High investment may be needed if the RPL system is in its early days to foster trust and credibility in the
system, encouraging more candidates and enterprises to participate. Over time, as RPL’s reputation grows
and the process becomes more widely known and understood, these costs will decrease.
Identification of cost elements
Information and awareness-raising are different from guidance and counselling (described in the next
subsection). The aim here is to inform potential candidates about opportunities, how to access them and
to encourage them to register or find out more about the process by highlighting the various advantages
of RPL.

CASE STUDY 1
RPL in the South African insurance industry
In South Africa's insurance industry, RPL is an essential process that acknowledges and recognizes
the skills and knowledge professionals have gained through formal, non-formal and informal means.
This approach is vital for enhancing career progression within the sector and ensuring regulatory
compliance with the FAIS Act, No. 37 of 2002. This law was greeted with concern in the industry
upon its introduction, as many financial advisers and enterprises were worried that failure to comply
with the Fit and Proper Determination of the regulatory act would lead to significant job losses and
income reduction in the insurance sector. The concept of RPL was not widely understood by the
industry at the time, who were also unfamiliar with the National Qualifications Framework (NQF).
As part of a large-scale, industry-wide RPL project, a national awareness campaign was undertaken
by the INSETA (Insurance Sector Education and Training Authority) to deliver a clear, simple message
and minimize the widespread confusion surrounding the FAIS Act and RPL. The campaign was
structured as a roadshow, consisting of a series of events held across the country to inform the
public about RPL. In collaboration with industry stakeholders and industry professional bodies and
members’ associations, the campaign sought to:
� Provide general information about the FAIS Act for new entrants into the sector;
� Explain the Fit and Proper Determination and the concepts of the NQF, quality assurance and
RPL;
� Introduce the RPL project and the processes of registration, learner support systems, the
e-learning portal and the assessment process.
Special features of the awareness campaign are listed below:
� All the industry professional bodies and members’ associations supported and participated in
the roadshows.
� The roadshows were interactive, which provided opportunities for Q&A sessions. Reviews after
the presentations by the project team identified frequently asked questions and decisions were
made on ways to improve the communication.
� Print and radio media coverage was generated with support from respected industry and other
national print media such as Personal Finance, the Financial Advisors News and the Financial
Mail.
� Radio slots were booked with regional radio stations to extend the message to rural areas, in all
provinces and in all South African languages.
� Brochures were printed and widely distributed at the roadshows and through the INSETA
regional coordinators, company Skills Development Facilitators (SDFs), professional bodies and
associations.
As a result of the campaign, the role of INSETA was clarified and information about the NQF, RPL
assessment and credit accumulation and transfer of credits was provided. In January 2004, INSETA
had 90,000 hits on its website, which was up from the 31,000 hits in December 2003. INSETA viewed
this as an indication of the awareness campaign’s success. Another success indicator was the
unexpected 11,584 financial advisers enrolled for the first national assessment due at the end of
March 2004 and the 5,000 financial advisers enrolling for the May 2004 RPL assessment.

2.1.2. Counselling and facilitation
The RPL methodology for assessing an individual’s skills is complex, with different possible outcomes.
Applicants require significant support and counselling before, during and after the various stages of the
process.
This component of RPL is typically resource-intensive. Indeed, each candidate’s prior learning journey
is unique, necessitating tailored advice on gathering and presenting evidence to meet qualification
standards. Different levels and types of awards or credentials (for example, certificates, diplomas, degrees)
may vary in complexity, influencing the level and intensity of support needed per applicant.
Identification of cost elements
Before they formally apply, potential candidates must first determine their eligibility for RPL and receive
detailed information about the process. RPL providers are responsible for outlining the qualifications and
skills levels required, along with the associated costs, time frames, potential outcomes and advantages
of pursuing RPL.
Although some of this information may have been conveyed during awareness campaigns, it is emphasized
at this stage to provide more detailed and tailored guidance to applicants, given their varied sources of
information. In Bangladesh, for example, most RPL candidates found out about RPL through friends and
relatives, meaning that they may require further detailed information before entering the RPL process.
Guidance can be offered one-on-one or in groups, either in person or online, depending on the context.
The role of RPL facilitators/counsellors is crucial in helping candidates to decide whether to apply for RPL,
for which qualification and at what level, as well as how to utilize their newly acquired credentials if they
are successful in the RPL process. Additionally, facilitators/counsellors guide candidates in understanding
and gathering the necessary evidence for their application, such as employment records, testimonials,
training certificates and work samples. This support is particularly valuable as applicants may possess the
required credentials but not know how to effectively showcase their learning experiences from various
contexts. Facilitators/counsellors work closely with candidates to explore these experiences, helping to
translate learning outcomes into tangible proof that supports their RPL application.
Once collected, the supporting documentation is submitted along with an application form. RPL providers
verify each application to ensure that all necessary information is compiled and can be reviewed for
assessment according to established RPL criteria.

2.1.3. Assessment and certification
Assessment and certification are the two most visible components of the RPL system. Both are visible
guarantees of the success of the validation process.
Assessment verifies that all the documents collected correspond to the skills expected for the targeted
qualification. The most common expected outcome of the assessment is a qualification. It can be a total
qualification, a partial one or a microcertification. Alternatives are local certificates of labour market
competences, including when provided by employers’ or workers’ associations, exemptions of academic
prerequisite for access to formal education and training and/or shortening of a curriculum (exemption of
part of the learning programme).
Assessing candidates' skills requires properly trained professionals and knowledge of qualification levels.
The jury (that is, panel of assessors) can be made up of several people from the private sector, education
and RPL experts.

CASE STUDY 3
The assessment process in Australia
The assessment system in Australia differs somewhat from what is often used in other RPL systems.
It is important to point out that RPL is practiced at national level, but each administrative department
uses its own RPL system. All providers’ processes are supervised by Australian Skills Quality Authority
(ASQA) who require that the principles of assessment and rules of evidence are applied. The cost
to the candidate varies according to where they live, the microcertificate or certificate they wish to
achieve, and whether they are one of the target groups to be financially assisted by the state.
What is special about Australia is that there is no identical procedure at national level. The assessment
process is entirely managed by the private and public providers, who are totally autonomous. There
are three stages: eligibility, collection of evidence by candidates and then assessment, which takes
place over a period of time that is specific to each application.
Candidates undergo an eligibility process whereby they are gauged as likely to meet the requirements
of the unit/s and this usually involves a self-assessment. Some departments have developed an
online platform for self-assessment. For other departments and for target audiences such as migrant
workers, people with no internet connection or those who are illiterate, this stage can be carried out
face-to-face.
The second stage, evidence gathering, is common to all countries involved in RPL. The third stage,
on the other hand, is highly specific. In Australia, candidates are supervised by an assessor who
accompanies them throughout the process, right up to the assessment. There is no final assessment
in front of a jury.
Each assessor can use the tools they wish, and there is no set assessment time. This may involve, for
example, analysis of documents provided by the candidate, a visit to the candidate’s workplace to
observe him or her perform his or her tasks or an interview with the candidate’s superior. Costs will
therefore vary according to the procedure chosen by the assessor.

2.1.4. Addressing skills gaps
Feedback by assessors on candidates’ shortcomings during the RPL process helps them identify and
address the gap between their skills and qualification standards. Many individuals require some form of
additional knowledge and skills to meet prescribed standards.
In some contexts (Brazil, Colombia, Tanzania, South Africa, Bangladesh), upskilling is a central objective
of RPL to help candidates obtain qualifications and raise skills standards in certain industries (top-up
training). In other places, RPL may focus solely on testing current skills without emphasizing upskilling.
Regardless of the approach, it is important to consider the costs of options to address skills gaps. Indeed,
RPL candidates seeking to upgrade or acquire new skills often face challenges, as education and training
systems may lack the capacity and flexibility to develop and deliver customized programmes that meet
their needs.
Identification of cost elements
The training design for upskilling programmes should be based on candidate’s skills gaps identified
either before or during the RPL assessment. For example, the Vocational Education and Training Authority
(VETA) in Tanzania targets apprentices in the informal sector through RPL. Before receiving their award,
successful candidates participate in free short-term skills upgrading courses offered during the summer,
aimed at supplementing their practical skills with theoretical knowledge and core skills.
While training curricula may be available to address gaps related to specific competence units of a
qualification, they often require adaptation to fit the needs of RPL candidates. Delivery of training should
consider the participant profile, including learning preferences, appropriate training methods for the skills
in question (for example, work-based learning, online learning), training schedules (some RPL candidates
are full-time workers) and resources and logistics.
Costing these activities involves accounting for the development and customization of training materials,
instructor fees, venue costs for in-person training, and digital infrastructure for virtual training
programmes.

2.2.2. Regulatory framework
A strong regulatory framework on RPL is important to ensure that the roles and responsibilities of
stakeholders are clear and that processes are consistent across institutions. Consistency builds the
credibility of the system and determines its success. Ultimately, a regulatory framework should support
the following objectives of an RPL system:11
� be an integral part of a country’s education and training system
� promote alternative pathways to acquiring qualifications, by ensuring parity with formal education
and training
� facilitate lifelong learning
� ensure stakeholder commitment and
� be sufficiently funded to ensure that the system works effectively and efficiently.
The development of a robust regulatory framework can be an incremental process, allowing for gradual
expansion and refinement over time. The costs associated with developing this framework will largely
depend on several factors, including the scope of RPL coverage and the extent of stakeholder consultations
required.

2.2.3. Institutional framework
A clearly defined and functional institutional framework is critical for the success of an RPL system.
Effective implementation starts by leveraging and strengthening existing institutions, structures and
resources, rather than building entirely new systems from scratch. Institutions tasked with the planning,
implementation and management of RPL have the responsibility to operationalize the provisions of the
RPL regulatory framework and coordinate with other relevant stakeholders.
Trust in RPL depends on the front-line personnel and professionals directly involved with candidates –
strengthening their capacity requires ongoing investment to maintain quality over time. Even countries
with a good recognition system have expressed their inability to rapidly scale up RPL due to lack of
competent staff and/or facilities.
Identification of cost elements
In most RPL systems, overall responsibility for implementation is assigned to one authority. Depending
on the regulatory framework, this body may be an existing public institution (for example, Ministry of
Labour in Colombia, Bangladesh Technical Education Board, Kenya National Qualifications Authority…) or
a professional or sectoral body covering RPL (for example, the Nursing and Midwifery Council in the UK).
Administrative costs incurred by this authority in coordinating RPL implementation will vary according to
the size and scope of the RPL system, the complexity of the processes involved and the specific needs of
the regulating authority.
Under the coordination of the recognition authority, countries then often establish a series of “recognition
providers” responsible for RPL. These may include:
� professional bodies that assess and recognize qualifications in their field of expertise
� employers’ representatives
� industry councils
� training providers and boards
� regional/local authorities
RPL personnel usually combine their duties with other functions, for example in guidance, teaching and
training and/or work-management. However, RPL involves distinct procedures, tools and methods that
differ from standard assessments used in formal education and training. It also requires a specialized
approach to guidance and counselling, as candidates typically come from outside the education and
training environment.
Therefore, RPL professionals should be equipped with skills relevant to the different stages of the RPL
process. In Colombia for example, assessors are trained through general modules covering the national
qualifications framework and the RPL process, as well as specific modules focused on counselling
approaches and assessment methods. Personnel in Bangladesh and Kenya receive additional training on
the e-RPL platform used at all stages.
It is essential to carry out an analysis of existing institutional capacity in the first place, as well as future
capacity needs, to identify and address gaps that could affect implementation.

2.2.4. Quality assurance
Setting up a quality assurance system will ensure the successful implementation and long-term future
of RPL.
RPL is a new way of obtaining a qualification, which can be misunderstood by stakeholders. Setting up a
quality assurance system will help reinforce confidence in the qualifications thus obtained.
All stages of the RPL process must be thought through and adopted in such a way as to ensure that:
� The procedure is accessible to everyone,
� The procedure is standardized throughout the country or department (as in Australia), that is all
players offer the same service,
� Professionals and RPL centres are certified as complying with the procedure.
� A monitoring and evaluation system tracks key performance indicators, collects regular feedback,
assesses system progress and performance and makes adjustments if need be.
Quality assurance means continually evaluating and improving the RPL process to enhance its efficiency
and effectiveness. This may involve regular assessments of the various stakeholders to be able to evaluate
the system on a regular basis in order to maintain quality, including candidates.
Identification of cost elements
Investment in quality assurance for the procedure is essential and necessary. The associated costs are
high and will vary over time. Right from the start of the RPL system project, you need to think about setting
up quality standards that will be monitored and evaluated over time and modified if necessary to ensure
that the system remains efficient.
At each stage of the procedure, the quality of the system will have to be questioned. To implement quality
assurance, it is necessary to monitor and evaluate each stage and adapt them if required. To achieve this,
it is necessary to develop the continuous professional skills of practitioners.
Quality assurance in the context of RPL requires data to be collected, analysed and transformed into
usable information to ensure quality services. Collaborative work between the various stakeholders will
be highly beneficial, so that all parties can make their contribution. To facilitate this, it can be important
to rely on a regulatory framework. In the absence of such a framework, additional costs may be incurred
in setting it up (3.2.1).
In order to guarantee a reassuring framework for users, strong communication around the reliability
of the RPL system should be envisaged. To achieve this, well-trained personnel and accredited training
centres are essential. Evaluation of the system by the candidates themselves will also provide qualitative
and quantitative data that will enable RPL to be adapted to the socio-economic context.

� 2.3. Opportunity costs
2.3.1. Issues
Opportunity cost refers to the value of what is forgone when a choice is made. In the context of RPL, this
involves two levels of analysis: (1) the opportunity costs (economic and non-economic) for individuals
deciding whether to engage in RPL and (2) the national opportunity cost of creating and maintaining (or
not maintaining) an RPL system.
The opportunity costs for individuals considering an RPL process are an important consideration, as
potential applicants will inevitably factor them in. They may not be aware of the potential benefits and
alternatives they forego when they choose to engage in RPL, but designing supportive policies is critical
to try to balance the forgone benefits.
The first issue that comes to mind is the time investment by individuals. Time spent on RPL guidance,
preparation and assessments could have been used for paid work. For individuals who must take time off
from their jobs, this results in lost income. Individuals may also have to sacrifice personal or leisure time
to complete their RPL process and related activities. For all individuals, especially for those uncertain if
their knowledge and skills meet existing qualification standards, time and resources spent on RPL could
have been used for pursuing additional education and training that would also lead to a qualification and
also to new knowledge and skills, and (possibly) with a higher level of legitimacy because achieved in the
formal education and training system. In all the cases above, individuals might delay accepting job offers
or promotions if they are involved in an intensive RPL process.
After or together with, time costs, financial costs are critical because fees for enrolling in RPL could have
been used for other personal or even occupational investments. If individuals need to borrow money to
cover RPL expenses, they incur interest and potential debt, leading to additional stress and increased fear
of failure. This is a thorny issue because the idea of providing RPL for free creates new issues such as lack
of motivation and the feeling of investing time for a qualification that may not carry societal value and
currency in the labour market.
In addition to the “economic” stress mentioned above, the RPL process may cause mental and emotional
strain. The RPL process can be stressful, particularly because individuals can never be certain about the
outcomes and tend to underestimate their actual knowledge and skills. In addition, RPL applicants are not
used to assessment processes and they will necessarily face challenges in preparing the required evidence
and documentation. The final result is uncertain and, in addition, there is a risk that even successful RPL
applicants will not receive the desired recognition of their newly acquired qualification – there is evidence
in several countries where RPL is not known and not widely accepted – meaning their investment of time
and resources may not yield the expected benefits.
For all the reasons above, workers may see their work performance and family life negatively affected.
Enrolling in an RPL process may reduce the time and energy individuals can dedicate to their current job,
potentially impacting their performance and job security. In extreme cases, individuals might leave their
current jobs to focus on RPL, facing a period of unemployment and financial instability. Unemployed
people may find they have less energy (and less time) for job search activities. Time dedicated to RPL may
come at the expense of spending time with family and friends, affecting personal relationships and social
well-being. Individuals with caregiving responsibilities may need to find alternative care arrangements,
leading to additional costs and logistical challenges.
Beyond the individual level, it is important to consider the national opportunity costs of establishing and
maintaining an RPL system or conversely, the costs of not investing in one. Governments and policymakers

must weigh the potential economic and social gains from RPL against the investments required to develop,
administer and promote the system. While the specific benefits will depend on the goals of each RPL
system, governments can assess at least the following two indicators against the identified costs to
evaluate their return on investment:
� Enhanced economic participation: By recognizing the skills of marginalized groups, such as
migrant workers, informal workers or those with interrupted education, RPL can help integrate these
individuals into the formal economy, contributing to higher employment rates and tax revenues.
� Reduced public spending on education: RPL allows individuals to bypass unnecessary portions of
formal education, reducing the burden on public education and training systems. This can lower the
cost of delivering qualifications and free up resources to be used elsewhere.
2.3.2. Mitigating opportunity costs
Mitigating opportunity costs for individuals is difficult because some of the risks – typically risk of
underperforming in the RPL assessment process – are not necessarily real. Having said that, this also
implies that several of these risks may be dispelled through an appropriate communication policy and
individual guidance, backed up by concrete evidence that RPL leads to enhanced employment outcomes
(provided through LMIS).
To reduce economic opportunity costs, policymakers and RPL providers can implement several supportive
measures.
Offering flexible schedules and part-time options to accommodate working individuals, job seekers and
all those with high demand for free time and/or family responsibilities is one of them. Financial support
of all kinds (for example, direct grant or payment plan) is also clearly an option, especially for those at
the bottom of the earning distribution, those delivering unpaid care work and/or those have to support
a family. This approach is particularly recommended when well-off individuals are among the applicants,
since the financial support could be means-tested and therefore be equitable. Worthy of note is that,
in some countries for some potential RPL applicants, even a symbolic fee to enter the RPL process is
sometimes a heavy burden on the family.
When RPL applicants are employees, encouraging their employers to support them through paid leave,
financial contributions and flexible work arrangements is also an option. Organizing a positive rhetoric
within enterprises is of paramount importance. As said above, providing a comprehensive information and
guidance system is also essential, typically for ensuring that RPL qualifications are widely recognized in
the society, valued by employers and have currency in the labour market. Offering clear guidance before,
during and after the RPL process is key. Finally, elaborating a convincing and visible quality assurance
system and maintaining high standards for RPL is critical.

Designing financial arrangements for RPL is often a priority for policymakers when establishing an RPL
system because there is little hindsight from international experience, as the upscaling of RPL is rather
recent in most countries. It is however critical for several reasons. An appropriate design of financial
arrangements has the potential to have positive impacts on accessibility as well as on inclusivity – and
therefore on take-up –, on sustainability and on equity.
It is critical for accessibility and inclusivity because financial barriers have proven an issue in many similar
circumstances (for example, undertaking adult learning activities, enrolling in a qualification process in
general). Lowering the costs of RPL for individual stakeholders – typically learners and enterprises – is
therefore a specific objective to reduce the cost burden on them and therefore making RPL accessible to
a broader population, including low-income groups and vulnerable populations. Another consideration,
for increased accessibility and inclusivity, relates to flexible payment plans. Offering the options of paying
with several deferred instalment, subsidies or voucher systems can help individual stakeholders afford
the cost of RPL (section 3.2.3). Finally, increasing take up may not directly depend on the amount of money
invested in RPL but rather on the way it is spent. Effective financial arrangements may be designed in a
way to reach out to those who are the most in need for RPL – and may not be aware of this opportunity –
because they do not have the financial means or are underrepresented, such as asylum seekers, forcibly
displaced persons, migrant workers in general and those who constitute the typical target group of RPL,
that is, those with no qualifications from the national formal education and training system.
It is critical for the sustainability of RPL programmes. Ensuring long-term viability means stable funding
sources. This may be achieved by establishing reliable and diverse funding sources, including public,
private and donor contributions. Again, experience suggests – especially for the pilot phase – that funding
arrangements have been unsustainable when they relied only on international organizations and donors.
In addition, careful planning in relation to financing RPL is necessary to help design cost-effective models.
This means aiming for convincing results without spending too much money. In the case of RPL, cost-
effectiveness is an important consideration as experience shows that countries have been more or less
demanding in the final assessment process for example, with assessments ranging from a single day to an
entire week. Sustainability may also be the consequence of continuous improvement which, in turn, relies
on funding and quality assurance. Adequate funding allows for continuous improvement in the quality
of RPL assessments, training of assessors and updating of standards. Finally, even if RPL may not need
investment in infrastructure, these Guidelines aim at being comprehensive and therefore also touches on
financial support, if necessary, for developing and maintaining the infrastructure required for developing
and maintaining an RPL system. From this perspective, it is also interesting to learn from existing systems
because different countries have different aims for building their RPL system, so resourcing it must be
tailored to these specific goals. As stated on several occasions, specific RPL infrastructure may not be
necessary if the RPL leaders are able to strike a deal with owner of existing facilities in order to piggyback
on their facilities (for example, training centres whose facilities are not used in the evening or on Saturday
morning).
It is critical for equity and social justice. Beyond the specific issue of accessibility, adequate financial
arrangements can help address inequalities by providing support to disadvantaged groups, ensuring
they have equal opportunities for success in the RPL process and not only equal access. RPL is also a
powerful driver of social upward mobility, as it is accessible to populations most in need of a qualification
or any related award (for example, credits, exemption for access, shortening of the curriculum). Beyond
economic benefits, RPL has the potential for providing successful applicants with larger benefits such
as increased self-esteem and increased sense of agency. Adequate financial arrangements are also
critical for ensuring a fair assessment of all learning outcomes of all applicants. Experience suggests that
assessments demand a thorough quality assured process and adequate funding helps with developing
standardized assessment tools and practices, valid across entire countries for reliability, validity, fairness
and consistency of the assessment process. Quality assurance is a strong predictor of legitimacy,

Guidelines on costing and financing recognition of prior learning (RPL) 57
credibility and acceptance of RPL awards. It somewhat relies on the budget dedicated to quality assurance
mechanisms.
It is critical to the consistency of the public policy in education and work. RPL is oftentimes introduced
under the lifelong learning (LLL) umbrella. There are good reasons for that, considering the value of RPL
as a stepping stone to further learning activities, whether formal or not. Nevertheless, without convincing
funding mechanisms, RPL and LLL often remain parallel disconnected routes to qualifications. Not only
adequate funding mechanisms may promote RPL as such but designing connections between resources
for LLL and resources for RPL will create a multiplication effect because of multiplied opportunities, let
alone the simplicity for end users when money available for LLL can also be used for RPL. Finally, RPL can
promote a culture of lifelong learning, motivating individuals to continue updating and upgrading their
skills.
Finally, designing financial arrangements, is critical for governance purposes. This design phase is indeed
an opportunity to align national policy aims with one another. Too often several layers of policies are
created over time and across sectors (for example, different sectors of the LLL system, education versus
labour) and there is no consistency in the support to the implementation of different policies even when
they work in the same directions (for example, qualification of the workforce, improved integration in
the labour market, addressing the needs of the vulnerable groups). When adequately designed, financial
arrangements for RPL can help align national education/training and employment policies and therefore
boost the social and economic agenda of the country.
It is difficult to assess why some countries are successful in implementing RPL, but it seems quite clear
that those who have a vision are more often successful. For instance, when the link between integration
in the labour market, informal apprenticeship, informal learning, qualifications or other forms of award
and all the components of a qualifications system (OECD 2007) are considered as a whole, countries are
more successful because, among others, the buy-in by stakeholders is more rapid and more sustained.
In fact, it is difficult to disentangle whether effective governance requires adequate financing
arrangements or whether it is the other way around. On one side the line of argument developed above
pleads for the financial arrangement design phase being an opportunity to streamline governance.
Nevertheless, it remains true that properly designed financial arrangements also require effective
governance structures to ensure transparency, accountability and efficient use of the resources made
available to RPL. In addition, well-structured financial arrangements can attract international donors and
organizations to support RPL initiatives and use existing funds more effectively. Leveraging international
aid is critical in a context where national resources are scarce.
� 3.1. Allocation mechanisms
In the process of designing RPL financing arrangements and optimal allocation mechanisms, the debate
over demand-side versus supply-side funding inevitably arises. The issue revolves around the most
effective way to allocate resources to ensure that RPL meets key criteria such as accessibility, inclusivity,
high take up, sustainability, quality and equity.
The debate between demand-side and supply-side funding for RPL is critical because both approaches
have pros and cons, and that they are not mutually exclusive. Therefore, the decision-making process
is not about choosing one or the other but about the breakdown between the two. The ideal solution
indeed often lies in a balanced, hybrid approach that leverages the strengths of both while addressing
their weaknesses. The final decision has an impact on effective governance, robust policy frameworks and
context-specific strategies, which are key to the success and sustainability of RPL.

3.1.1. Demand-side funding
Demand-side funding allocates resources directly to individual RPL applicants or to enterprises for them
to send their employees to RPL.
When it comes to individual applicants, a direct fund allocation to them means they have the final word in
deciding what to do (for example, qualification aimed, RPL provider chosen). This can include individual
learning accounts, vouchers, grants or loans. The first advantage is that it provides the individual applicants
with agency about the RPL process and their personal or professional aspirations. Individual learners can
best select the RPL programme according to their needs, preferences and circumstances, leading to a
more personalized RPL experience (for example, RPL for access to formal education as opposed to RPL for
achieving a full-fledged qualification). There are advantages and disadvantages to individual demand-side
funding. On the one hand, providers are incentivized to improve the quality of their services and innovate
to attract more applicants. On the other hand, it can be a disadvantage because providers may use unfair
approaches to promote their business (for example, misleading advertisement, fake maximization of
number of clicks on platform for appearing first when using search engine). Demand-side funding is
that it facilitates the targeting of groups that are at the top of the policy agenda. Combined with tailored
support, this may in turn increase accessibility and access.
When it comes to enterprises who invest in RPL services for their employees, a direct fund allocation
to them also means that they have a direct control of the qualifications that are targeted. It is clearly
beneficial to them, both in terms of internal human resources management – as skills become fully visible
and job matching easier – and for succeeding in attracting investors or bidding for more lucrative contracts
(for example, public contracts). The benefit of RPL to the enterprise also comes from the fact that RPL
helps in formalizing and valuing employees’ existing skills, which enhances productivity and ensures the
workforce meets industry standards and regulations. Enterprises are incentivized to pay for RPL services
when the financing system is conducive to doing it, such as the countries where employers contribute to
training levies (for example, Sub-Saharan Africa) because they can claim back funds or receive subsidies
for sending their employees to RPL.
From the perspective of both individual learners and enterprises, demand-side funding offers flexibility,
provided that it doesn’t come with excessive rules. If the system remains relatively flexible, despite the
direct funding as it were, individual applicants can use it at any accredited provider and for any qualification
registered with the national qualifications framework.
Finally, demand-side funding is inherently demand-driven, meaning that the RPL system is resourced to
respond to the actual needs of learners, potential RPL applicants and employers. This can be a challenge
as there is no guarantee that individual learners know their needs and, if they do, which solutions will best
address them. This can be easily addressed by imposing some sort of guidance – by RPL personnel – as
part of the “contract” to receive an RPL grant. It is also a thorny issue for enterprises, which may prioritize
short-term needs when they buy RPL services, potentially hindering progress towards broader goals for
the sector, the region and even the country (for example, green and digital twin transitions). This can also
be addressed by helping enterprises to identify their mid-term needs in addition to their short-term ones.
More difficult to control are disadvantages in terms of potential inequities. Indeed, only individuals and
enterprises with access to adequate information are likely to apply for grants and buy into RPL, raising the
ongoing debate about whether RPL truly addresses the needs of those most in need of skills visibility and
recognition or the layer just above, that has access to the necessary information. The issue of reaching out
to the neediest is a significant concern across all countries, whether they have an advanced RPL system
or an embryonic one. Even among those individuals or enterprises that have access to resources, some

Guidelines on costing and financing recognition of prior learning (RPL) 59
may have access to better information than others about the best RPL providers (for example, in term of
achievement rates or quality of the assessment).
Demand-side funding may also prove complex to manage, creating a need for robust administrative
systems. Finally, there is a risk that the money allocated to some individuals/enterprises is misused,
especially if there is collusion between individual learners or enterprises and RPL providers. This highlights
the need for effective monitoring and control mechanisms.
3.1.2. Supply-side funding
Supply-side funding allocates resources directly to RPL providers or institutions and organizations
promoting RPL. Supply-side funding is therefore more about the provision of RPL services. Although
enterprises may directly provide RPL services – this is happening on a small scale in France for example
– to their own employees (for example, providing infrastructure and assessors), for the purpose of this
document, enterprises are not considered to be on the supply-side, for the reasons provided above that
they are mainly meant to buy RPL services.
The first advantage of supply-side funding is the consistency since RPL providers can maintain consistent
standards and quality across their services, leading to reliable and credible RPL outcomes, therefore
running less risks of creating major disappointments among RPL applicants and among enterprises hiring
RPL graduates. Higher quality will also be achieved because sustained funding may help providers to plan
their investments and therefore constantly invest in improving the services provided to RPL applicants
(for example, infrastructure, staff). Capacity-building among the RPL providers is made easier in the case
of supply-side funding.
Another advantage of supply-side funding is its simplicity. Indeed, direct funding to providers means there
are a lot less stakeholders involved in the process and administration and control are easier. The risk of
misuse is less present when only accredited institutions receive the funds.
As expected, the main disadvantages of supply-side funding fall on individuals. They have fewer choices,
including in terms of RPL providers, which can reduce the personalized nature of RPL programmes. They
may also be faced with providers not very motivated to improve the service they provide because they are
in a monopolistic position in a given geographic area.
Finally, supply-side funding is not demand driven and so inherently less oriented towards the actual needs
of learners and potential RPL applicants. In addition, funded providers may have to find solutions to cater
for the needs of disadvantaged groups (for example, vulnerable groups or groups in remote geographical
areas).
3.1.3. Combining demand-side and supply-side funding
As stated above, the debate between demand-side and supply-side funding revolves around the optimal
balance in a particular context to maximize the benefits of the RPL system. Combining both funding
mechanisms can indeed leverage the strengths of each approach while mitigating their weaknesses. For
example, core funding can be provided to ensure quality and infrastructure (supply-side), while individuals
grants can be provided to individual learners and/or enterprises to promote choice and competition
(demand-side), as well as social inclusion.

The respective proportions of demand-side and supply-side funding should consider the specific context,
including the maturity of the RPL system, the diversity of providers and the needs of the target population,
along with the labour market demand and skills gaps. For instance, in a well-developed RPL system with
many high-quality providers, demand-side funding might be more effective. In contrast, in emerging
systems, supply-side funding could help build capacity and ensure quality.
In any case, effective policy and governance frameworks are essential to ensure that both funding
mechanisms work effectively. This includes establishing clear rules for allocating funds and robust
monitoring system for quality assurance, as well as providing guidance to RPL applicants to help them
make informed decisions.
France is a good example of a combination of demand- and supply-side approaches. Indeed, on one side,
the individual learning account (CPF) can be mobilized for individuals to directly receive credits that they
can use for RPL, among others, on the ground that the government wants the individuals to be in charge
of their future (personal choice and flexibility). On the other side, the Regional Councils and Employment
Centres may direct funding to RPL centres to ensure consistent and high-quality services.

3.2.1. Government funding to individuals, enterprises or RPL providers
The government may subsidize RPL directly or indirectly (see section 4.1 for details about the demand- and
supply-side approaches). It may provide grants or loans to individuals or subsidies to RPL providers and/
or to enterprises for them to register their employees for an RPL process. In general, government funding
is an effective tool to make the case for RPL and create interest among stakeholders. When the official
rhetoric is backed up by adequate funding, the leverage effect is stronger. Government funding is used by
individual applicants to cover the cost of registration, if any, guidance and assessments. There is barely any
evidence of grants to cover opportunity costs and foregone earnings. This is critical in countries where not
working often means no earnings for covering the basic costs of life (for example, food, accommodation).
This is of critical interest since most RPL applicants are required to demonstrate experience even before
registration – eligibility phase, let alone actual assessment of learning outcomes – and therefore are adults
and have a family to sustain.

For example, in Australia, the Government funds RPL in the specific niche of Skills Recognition and Training

through a national qualification as part of their transition to the civilian workforce. They qualify for up to

very small component of the Australian RPL system. Generally speaking, in Australia, the funding is mainly
private and, when it is by government, States vary in their contractual arrangements and percentage of
funding RPL. In South Africa, various government programmes and subsidies are available to support
RPL for critical skills shortages. In Kenya, the Government offers subsidized RPL services through the
Technical and Vocational Education and Training Authority (TVETA). In France, in addition to the CPF that is
an individual tool (Box 4a), the French Public Employment Service may provide up to €1,200 for a maximum
of three attempts at achieving a qualification – including three different qualifications at the same time if
they are of different level – over a period of five years. This covers the registration and assessment costs
and, mainly, the cost of guidance that is seen as critical for individual success. It is available to migrant
workers.
Government funding to individuals
Governments have several options for subsidizing individuals to encourage their enrolment in RPL.
These options vary according to the country's education/training, competences development and
lifelong learning systems and policies. They typically aim to make RPL more accessible and affordable for
individuals.
Governments can provide direct financial support to individuals in the form of subsidies or grants to
cover the costs of RPL assessments, related fees or top up learning programmes. For example, in South
Africa, the National Skills Fund (NSF) provides grants to individuals to cover the cost of RPL assessments,
especially for workers in priority sectors. In Australia, in some cases, individuals can apply for grants under
specific competences development programmes or regional initiatives that fund RPL.
Governments can also issue vouchers or education/RPL credits to individuals that they can use to pay
for RPL assessments or related learning activities. These vouchers can be redeemed by accredited RPL
providers. The French individual learning account (CPF) is the typical example of money transferred to
individuals for specific use, here RPL (Box 4a).
The loan system, largely experimented in the case of education and training, is also an option whereby
individuals receive funds for their RPL process that they have to pay back when they reach a certain level
of incomes. In Australia, the VET Student Loans programme may be used by eligible individuals to cover
RPL costs in some VET programmes. In the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, the
Advanced Learner Loan may be used to cover RPL for certain qualifications.
When RPL systems are in their infancy, it is quite frequent that the funding of national RPL systems (as
opposed to sectoral systems) – especially in relation to the piloting of RPL systems – relies entirely on
the government (for example, Colombia). This is partly due to the lack of knowledge of key stakeholders
regarding the existence of RPL and to the need for the government to start the process quickly. This is
common when RPL is at the top of the policy agenda (for example, Kenya, South Africa) and/or quite
consensual among key stakeholders (for example, Kenya again, with Jua Kali quite active in the field). In
this case, it is basically a subsidy to individuals. Enterprises and RPL providers are not involved in receiving
the funds or spending them on RPL.

Government funding to enterprises (for their own employees)
It is not a standard practice for governments to directly subsidize private enterprises for sending their
employees to RPL. The extent and nature of such subsidies vary from country to country, depending on
their skills development policies and lifelong learning frameworks and it seems they are closely connected
to the existence of a levy system. For example, South Africa has a structured system where employers
contribute to the Skills Development Levy (SDL), which is administered by Sector Education and Training
Authorities (SETAs). Enterprises that send their employees to RPL programmes can claim grants or
subsidies from SETAs, making the government a significant funder of enterprise-driven RPL initiatives.
Additionally, the National Skills Fund (NSF) provides funding for priority skills development programmes,
which often include RPL. In Kenya, while direct funding to enterprises for RPL is limited, employers who
contribute to the Industrial Training Levy can receive training reimbursements from NITA, including for
RPL-related activities. This is indirect financial support but targeted at enterprises sending their employees
to RPL.
Government funding to RPL providers (for their RPL applicants)
It is even more rare that governments directly subsidize RPL providers, especially when these only offer
RPL services – as opposed to universities or TVET centres typically, which also offer formal learning
programmes and therefore qualifications through the formal learning route.
Yet, governments can offer subsidies directly to RPL providers for them to reduce the fees they charge
individuals for RPL assessments, in order to increase take up for example. This approach may not have
an impact on opportunity costs but clearly reduces the financial burden on individuals by lowering the
upfront costs of RPL (for example, registration fees, assessment fees). For example, in Singapore, the
government subsidizes training providers under the SkillsFuture initiative, which includes subsidies for
RPL.
Whether it concerns individuals, enterprises or RPL providers, an interesting approach to consider
would be through the income tax system. There is little evidence about this approach but, for example,
tax deductions or rebates may concern individual applicants who fully or partly pay for their own RPL
programme. This would be particularly effective for middle-income earners who can claim back the cost of
RPL. Indeed, low-income individuals generally do not pay tax and high-income ones are barely sensitive to
cost issues. In Canada, some provinces allow individuals to claim tax deductions for tuition and education-
related costs, including RPL assessments. In the United States of America, although it is not specific to RPL
either, some states offer tax credits for lifelong learning, which can cover RPL.
3.2.2 Enterprise sponsoring
Many employers support RPL as part of their professional development programmes. They may directly
cover the costs of RPL for their employees, in which case it is a win-win situation because individuals
achieve a qualification (for example, to contribute to their occupational project) and employers may access
more profitable markets (for example, when bidding for a tender that requires a certain proportion of the
labour force to have a qualification).
Enterprises may see additional interest in sponsoring their employees, because self-esteem and
confidence – which are well-documented gains of the RPL process as a whole, in addition to the awarding
of a qualification – are good predictors of increased productivity. This is why direct sponsoring to cover
registration fees, guidance and other related costs are often supplemented by additional help (for
example, flexible working arrangements).
At the time of the drafting and especially considering the countries under study, employers are not
yet too aware about the potential benefits for them and it is rare that they facilitate the undertaking of
an RPL process by their employee during working hours, at the workplace or elsewhere (for example,
Bangladesh).
In Kenya and South Africa, employers play a significant role in financing RPL for their employees.

3.2.3 Individual payments
Individuals oftentimes finance their RPL assessments directly, partly or fully. This is obviously common
where government or employer support is not available or not available at the necessary level (section
4.3.1). For example, in Australia, RPL is mainly privately funded (Box 4b)
In the case of employees, even with the most generous employers, individuals are on their own when the
qualification they are aiming for through RPL is outside the scope of their work. In the case of unemployed
people, when the public employment service (PES) contributes partly or fully, the aimed qualification has
to be relevant to the needs of the labour market in almost all cases.
When individuals are paying for themselves, there are sometimes opportunities to pay the cost of RPL
over time, through different instalments.
3.2.4 Donors’ financing
Even if it is rather rare, not-for-profit and community organizations may directly help some groups – at-
risk groups typically – to engage in an RPL process. It is rare because usually these organizations rather
focus on providing basic skills for their beneficiaries to make a living and usually use fast-track initiatives
to train individuals. This is often the case in post conflict situations (for example, Uganda). Nevertheless,
it is also true that RPL is sometimes considered a solution in post conflict situations, for example in
Colombia and was explicitly used for redress in South Africa after the end of the Apartheid. Armenia is
also a good recent example of international organizations intervening in emergency situations. This case
is interesting because several international organizations have grouped for a more impactful action: the
World Bank, various UN agencies, GIZ, the Asian Development Bank, the European Union and local NGOs
have gathered with various Armenian ministries for designing a Skills Recognition Action Plan for forcibly
displaced persons from the Karabakh region.
Generally speaking, when international organizations and donors help financing the RPL system, it is
usually at the macro level in order to set the RPL system in motion, hence a quasi-systematic focus on
governance or preparation work and pilot projects (for example, Bangladesh, Colombia, Tunisia). In this
case, they quite often stress the integration of RPL into national skills strategies and it is often taking
place in developing regions. These organizations do not only focus only on RPL, but RPL is quite often
among the instruments they promote, together with improving access to education, promoting workforce
development and addressing skills gaps, often targeting disadvantaged or marginalized groups. For
example, the World Bank with the Skills and Training Enhancement Project (STEP) in Bangladesh, with a
focus on the upskilling of workers in the informal economy, with the support of the Canadian Government.
In Ghana, the Skills Development Fund (SDF) has included RPL as part of its strategy to make visible
the skills of workers in the informal economy. The European Social Fund of the European Union plays a
significant role in subsidizing RPL as part of its development cooperation programmes in member states
(for example, the VAE in France, RPL in Ireland and EVC in the Netherlands). In Tunisia, the European Union
also cofinance programmes since the mid 2010 where RPL is quite present (PEFESE, Programme d’appui

Financing instruments for RPL encompass various mechanisms and tools used to fund RPL initiatives. They
are not specific to RPL and may be found in the TVET sector typically.
3.3.1. Levy system
A levy system involves mandatory contributions from employers to a centralized fund dedicated to RPL,
among other instruments linked to skills development and access to qualifications. The fund would
possibly have different targets and funding mechanisms and RPL would be among the targets, together
with skills development for instance. The levy is typically calculated as a percentage of the employer’s
payroll and is collected to create a pooled fund that supports RPL. The primary aim of a levy is to ensure
a sustained source of funding.
The key feature of a training levy is that all employers are required by law to contribute a specified
percentage of their payroll towards the levy. The contribution rate can vary depending on the country and
the specific regulations in place. It is usually connected to the size of the enterprises, with small enterprises
generally exempted. It may happen that enterprises already investing in RPL are also exempted from this
contribution (enterprises do RPL or pay tax).
Another feature is that the collected levies are pooled into a central fund managed by a government agency
or a dedicated RPL development authority. This fund is then used to finance various RPL programmes or
components of the RPL system (for example, training assessors, communication strategy).
Funds from the levy are earmarked for specific purposes and only the RPL system could benefit from
this money. Having said that, the levy approach leaves all latitude to government to focus the funding on
policy target groups or other issues (for example, addressing skills shortages, improving the overall level
of qualification of the workforce, reducing inequities and promoting decent wages/jobs).

3.3.2. RPL fund
Again, a fund is not a specific instrument. A fund is a dedicated financial resource established to support
the development and enhancement of a particular aspect (that is, skills within the workforce). In the
case of RPL, it is typically funded through contributions from employers, government allocations or other
sources, and is managed by a regulatory or oversight body.
A fund is an interesting instrument because it pools resources that are set aside for a specific purpose (here
RPL). It therefore allows for the allocation of resources toward a clearly defined aim or specific objectives.
It ensures that resources are used efficiently and for the intended purpose. It enables key stakeholders
to focus resources on the most critical areas of concern (for example, marginalized groups, labour force,
skills recognition). A fund makes it easier to track where money is being spent. Since the resources are
allocated for a specific purpose, there is usually more transparency on how the money is used, leading to
better financial management and accountability, therefore creating trust, which is not always there yet
when it comes to RPL. A fund ensures that the provision of resources is rather stable over time, supporting
long-term aims, such as going beyond the pilot(s). If properly managed, a fund can attract investments
and donations, for example, involving the private sector and international donor in something that is
always mainly seen as the responsibility of the Government. Because it is earmarked, a fund ensures that
resources are not dispersed across unrelated projects or initiatives, preventing fragmentation of efforts
and ensuring that critical areas receive the attention they need.
A fund is not an instrument that is specific to RPL. It is used way beyond the field of education and training.
In the TVET sector, it is amply used in French-speaking Africa for example (Gauron and Walther 2006).
Except in Australia, there is no known example of a fund that would be dedicated to RPL only. In practice,
all existing funds are meant to fund skills development in general, with RPL one of the possible types of
expenditure.
At the time of drafting, the possible reasons why dedicated RPL funds do not exist on a large scale is
probably because RPL is often considered in a wider context, such as lifelong learning or more narrowly
within the context of substituting end-of-apprenticeship exams for apprentices in the informal economy or
equivalence for TVET qualifications. It is rarely seen as a stand-alone initiative. This perspective overlooks
the fact that an RPL system is somewhat easy to set in motion, with quick wins for all stakeholders, especially
individuals and enterprises, whereas qualifications frameworks, for example, can be cumbersome and
politically risky. Having said that, it is also true that RPL is just another route to qualifications making it is
difficult to justify a special treatment.
RPL is often perceived as an administrative process rather than a standalone tool, which means it does
not always benefit from a strong political support. This may be due to a lack of understanding or a poor
communication strategy. Finally, in some countries, there is a belief that RPL will receive limited demand
and does not cost much. The lack of monitoring and evaluation also contribute to these beliefs as there is
no real measurement of the benefits of RPL, hence it is difficult to justify its cost and its financing through
a specific fund. In some countries, the emerging belief that the solution to skills deficit is quick training
does not help RPL, which requires more time to organize. Where opportunities to address skills gaps are

As seen above, RPL funds are often financed through a levy system whereby employers contribute a

3.3.3. Loans
It is also possible to provide loans to individual applicants for enrolling in an RPL programme and examples
can be found in Australia and South Africa. It is standard practice for studying in the higher education
system, but little evidence of wide use could be found for RPL in the countries under study. There is for
example the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) in South Africa that is primarily aimed at
covering costs for higher education, but which is said to be able to also support TVET and RPL (see the
National Student Financial Aid Scheme). In Australia, the Government offers loans to eligible students to
cover the cost of their TVET or RPL.18
Given the potential of RPL to generate additional earning, thanks to the newly acquired qualification,
this is an option to consider. It is close in essence to a direct grant to individual applicants except that the
candidate will have to reimburse the sum they received. The repayment terms are usually spelled out in
the contract.
Loans typically need to be repaid after the individual completes their RPL and starts earning above a
certain income threshold, with terms varying by country or region. Most of these loan programmes also
have specific eligibility criteria, including residency status, income levels and the type of RPL programme.
This approach is nonetheless difficult to implement in countries with large informal economies since
income threshold cannot be monitored.
Examples of loans for RPL can be found in New Zealand, where the Student Loan Scheme offers student
loans for tertiary education, which can include costs associated with RPL processes. These loans are
designed to help students cover their tuition fees, course-related costs and living expenses while they
study. They can also be found in the United Kingdom, where Advanced Learner Loans are available to
individuals aged 19 and over to help cover the costs of approved qualifications at levels 3 to 6, which
can include RPL programmes. In Canada, various provincial student aid programmes, such as OSAP in
Ontario or StudentAid British Colombia, provide loans and grants that can be used for post-secondary
education and training, including RPL-related expenses. In the United States, while again primarily for
degree programmes, federal student aid can sometimes be used for vocational training and RPL if the
programme is offered by an eligible institution. In Norway, the Norwegian State Educational Loan Fund
(Lånekassen) provides loans and grants for students in higher education and vocational training, including

3.4.1. Role of governance structures in financing RPL – The need for
social dialogue
The governance structure has a major influence on the financing arrangements for RPL, mainly because
it will change its nature and therefore its cost. The organizational and regulatory frameworks within a
country's skills recognition system impact the funding and financial management of RPL initiatives.
First and foremost, national policies set objectives and priorities for skills development and RPL, therefore
influencing how funds are allocated to various programmes and systems, in turn influencing RPL financing
arrangements. In addition, according to whether these national policies lead to short- or long-term
strategies, the RPL financial arrangements should be designed to be sustained over time or not.
The bodies overseeing RPL may have different rules and regulations about the way to use the available
money for RPL, therefore influencing how efficient are the RPL system. For example, in South Africa,
Sector Education and Training Authorities (SETAs), oversee the disbursement of funds and therefore help
manage costs and ensure that financial resources are used to address the needs of the target groups.
When the funding is centralized, due to the existence of national funds pool resources from various
sources – for example, employer levies, government contributions – to finance RPL, there is a more
strategic and potentially impactful use of funds.
When partnerships approaches are used, such as partnerships between the public sector and the private
productive sectors, there is a sharing of the financial risk and therefore key stakeholders may be keener on
developing further the RPL system. In addition, through collaborative efforts, partnerships can leverage
resources more efficiently, reducing overall costs and enhancing the quality of the RPL programmes.
The involvement of employers – either directly or through a partnership as mentioned above – may make
useful usual financial incentives such as tax breaks. The involvement of employers generally has an impact
on the RPL financing arrangements because their investment can offset public sector costs and increase
the availability of RPL opportunities.
The presence of international organizations and donors in the governance may also have an impact on
RPL financial arrangements through the targeting of specific groups of the population that usually comes
with international funds.
The level of control in general – through quality assurance, elaboration of standards and/or monitoring
and evaluation – it also clearly has an impact on RPL financial arrangements. Although they have a limited
impact on the cost of RPL, they do imply a rethinking of the balance in the allocate of funds between
systemic costs and field costs.
If all stakeholders are involved in the governance structure, they likely all have a say in determining the
price to be paid by RPL participants – individuals and enterprise typically – which in turn impacts on the
RPL financial arrangements.
All these arguments militate in favour of social dialogue. RPL, perhaps more than other approaches,
requires that most stakeholders of the world of education and most stakeholders of the world of work
talk to one another.

Recognizing the diverse costs and funding structures of RPL, approaches that aim at sharing the cost of
RPL among the different stakeholders have the potential to create consensus. This involves contributions
from the Government, employers, RPL providers, individuals and international organizations and donors.
The debate about sharing the cost of RPL among key stakeholders revolves around finding the most
equitable, efficient and sustainable way to finance RPL initiatives.
In terms of equity, RPL serves the public good by improving workforce qualification levels and therefore
employability, which benefits society as a whole. Therefore, it should be partly publicly funded. This would
ensure that RPL is accessible to all, including unemployed people – through the public employment service
– and those from disadvantaged backgrounds who might not afford it otherwise. Since employers benefit
directly from a more qualified workforce, they should also contribute to covering of the cost of RPL for their
employees or employees in general. Individuals are also direct beneficiaries of the RPL system. Therefore,
they should also invest in their qualifications. If affordability is an issue, individuals could be invited to
pay a symbolic amount, but it seems always good to ask participants to pay because what is free has no
value and because motivation may be low if the price is nil. There are notable exceptions such as Colombia
where the economic crisis was so severe that the pilot has been delivered for free, at the expense of the
Government and with the support of IDB.
Another set of arguments relates to efficiency and effectiveness. Centralized funding and administration
of RPL can lead to economies of scale, reducing overall costs and increasing efficiency, which means that
the Government should contribute. Employers also should contribute so that they can tailor RPL to meet
specific qualifications needs, for them or in the labour market in general. Individuals who invest in their
own RPL may be more motivated to complete the process and they may have greater choice and flexibility
in selecting RPL providers and programmes that best meet their needs.
In terms of sustainability, the contribution of the Government guarantees sustainable funding models
that are necessary to ensure the long-term viability of RPL programmes. In order to contribute, employers
need assurance that their investment in RPL will yield tangible benefits, such as increased productivity and
reduced turnover. They may benefit from tax breaks and/or subsidies. Individuals need access to financial
support mechanisms, such as loans or grants, to make their contribution sustainable. Yet, investing in RPL
can lead to long-term career benefits, making it a sustainable personal investment.
In terms of accountability and transparency, governments have the potential to establish clear regulatory
frameworks to ensure transparency and accountability in the use of RPL funds. Regular monitoring and
evaluation of RPL programmes ensure that funds are used effectively and achieve desired outcomes.
Employers want to see measurable outcomes from their investment in RPL, requiring transparent
reporting and accountability mechanisms. Involving them in governance structures ensures that their
contributions are used appropriately and effectively. Individuals are more interested in clear information
about the costs, benefits and financial support options available for RPL. Transparency and accountability
protect individuals from potential exploitation by unscrupulous providers.
Establishing an effective and efficient RPL system requires careful planning and coordination, especially
in terms of financial arrangements. These guidelines are intended as a comprehensive tool to support
stakeholders in establishing an RPL system that effectively leverages financial resources in their own
national context. Rather than offering a list of prescriptions, they serve as a structured menu to ensure
that all aspects of financial planning and management are considered, giving the greatest chance to lead
to a sustainable and impactful RPL system.
Ultimately, the success of an RPL system depends on addressing critical factors such as needs assessment,
strategic planning and governance, funding and financial sustainability (including support to learners
and RPL applicants), stakeholder engagement and collaboration (social dialogue), quality assurance
and qualifications or assessment standards, awareness/communication and outreach, accessibility and
equity, innovation and adaptability, as well as monitoring and evaluation. By focusing on these pillars,
stakeholders can create an RPL system that not only optimizes financial resources but also unlocks
opportunities for individuals and contributes to national economic and social development.
